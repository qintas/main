Mental Health study in Tech industry

This research explores mental health trends in the tech industry using Open Source Mental Illness (OSMI) survey data from 2014 to 2019. The analysis focuses on employer size, workplace awareness initiatives, and the prevalence of diagnosed mental health conditions among employees.

The dataset relies on self-reported responses, which may introduce biases, such as overrepresentation of certain demographics or self-selection bias among respondents.

query = """
    SELECT
        a.QuestionID, 
        q.QuestionText AS questiontext,  
        a.SurveyID AS Year, 
        COUNT(DISTINCT a.UserID) AS respondents
    FROM Answer a
    JOIN Question q ON a.QuestionID = q.QuestionID
    GROUP BY a.QuestionID, q.QuestionText, a.SurveyID
    ORDER BY a.QuestionID, a.SurveyID;
"""

questionnaire = pd.read_sql(query, con)

Observations from first look at the survey¶

  • No data at all for 2015.
  • Question index 35-47 are missing in the dataset.
  • Questions 1-12 were asked in all 5 surveys (2014-2019).
  • Questions 1-35, 47-56 were asked in 4 consecutive surveys (2016-2019).
  • Questions 1-35, 47-89 were asked in 3 consecutive surveys (2017-2019).
  • Questions 90-118 were asked in 2014 or 2016. These are similar to the questions asked in later surveys, but wording a question differently does not provide a good unbiased way for comparison.
  • Questions 115-116 offer insight into specific ilness, but was only asked in 2016.

Key Columns for Analysis¶

  • SurveyID: Year of the survey.
  • Age: Respondent's age.
  • Gender: Gender of the respondent.
  • Country: Country of residence.
  • State: State (if applicable).
  • Sought_Treatment: Whether they sought mental health treatment (1 or 0).
  • Employer_Size: Size of the respondent's employer.
  • Employer_Tech: Whether the employer is in primarily Tech company (1 or 0; -1 if missing or unsure).
  • Employer_Initiative: Whether the respondent's employer has raised discussion about mental health.
  • MH_Diagnosed, MH_Conditions: Indicators diagnosed mental health conditions.
  • Position: Job position (if provided).

Data Bias and Representation Analysis

  • ✅ Right-leaning, normally distributed age of respondents centered around 30.
  • ✅ 70%+ male representation.
  • ✅ Over 60% of respondents are from the United States.

Potential Bias Concerns

  • ⚠️ Possible bias towards:
    • A tech worker.
    • Working in the United States.
    • In a tech company.
    • With increased likelihood of perceived or diagnosed mental health issues.
  • ⚠️ Gender disparity may be amplified due to the overrepresentation of the tech industry.
  • ⚠️ Known biases in the tech field may influence our observations.

Company Size and Industry Distribution

Key Observations

  • 📊 Our respondents generally work for a company within the Tech industry.
  • 📊 The dataset groups responses for company sizes "More than 1000" together, making it impossible to estimate precise sizes within this range.
  • 🏢 Most respondents in Tech work for companies with 26-100 employees, indicating a prevalence of smaller Tech firms.
  • 🏢 Outside of Tech, respondents primarily work for companies larger than 1000 employees.

Potential Implications

  • ⚖️ Larger Non-Tech companies may have a higher proportion of IT roles compared to smaller Non-Tech companies in the US.
  • 📢 The survey may have been more accessible to Tech employees in larger Non-Tech companies in the US than to those in smaller firms.

df_2016 = df[df["SurveyID"] == 2016].copy()

conditions = {
    "Anxiety Disorder (Generalized, Social, Phobia, etc)": "Anxiety",
    "Mood Disorder (Depression, Bipolar Disorder, etc)": "Mood",
    "Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder": "ADHD"
}

df_2016["MH_Conditions"] = df_2016["MH_Conditions"].astype(str)

for condition, short_name in conditions.items():
    df_2016[short_name] = df_2016["MH_Conditions"].str.contains(condition, regex=False, case=False)

role_counts = df_2016["Position"].value_counts()
mh_percentages, mh_ci_lower, mh_ci_upper = {}, {}, {}

for short_name in conditions.values():
    mh_role_counts = df_2016[df_2016[short_name]]["Position"].value_counts()
    proportion = mh_role_counts / role_counts
    mh_percentages[short_name] = (proportion * 100).fillna(0)

    standard_error = np.sqrt((proportion * (1 - proportion)) / role_counts)
    ci_margin = 1.96 * standard_error 

    mh_ci_lower[short_name] = ((proportion - ci_margin) * 100).clip(lower=0).fillna(0)
    mh_ci_upper[short_name] = ((proportion + ci_margin) * 100).fillna(0)

mh_role_df = pd.DataFrame(mh_percentages).sort_values(by=list(conditions.values()), ascending=False)
mh_ci_lower_df = pd.DataFrame(mh_ci_lower).reindex(mh_role_df.index)
mh_ci_upper_df = pd.DataFrame(mh_ci_upper).reindex(mh_role_df.index)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 7))
colors = {"Anxiety": "#4682B4", "Mood": "#FA8072", "ADHD": "#FFA500"}

for condition in conditions.values():

    ax.errorbar(

        yerr=[mh_role_df[condition] - mh_ci_lower_df[condition], mh_ci_upper_df[condition] - mh_role_df[condition]],

Summary & Final Thoughts

It appears that the larger the employer, the more likely it is that they invest extra effort in raising awareness about mental health.

However, a lower proportion of "I don't know" responses among employees of smaller companies suggests that respondents from larger organizations may find it harder to access mental health-related information. This could be due to information clutter and bureaucracy within large companies, but we will leave this for another research.

Understanding mental health in tech workplaces requires ongoing research, as both awareness and stigma evolve over time.
